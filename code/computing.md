What is a computer? At it's most basic level a computer is a machine, a physical
machine that does stuff when you poke it in the right places, or you crank the
handle, or whatever it is you use to make the machine work.

Over time standards have built up, specific ways of making computers, but it's
important to remember that they are physical machines responding to outside
stimula.

For example, how does a computer remember what time it is, even when it's turned
off? The answer of course is that it has a little tiny on clock on it with it's on
battery, so that even when the computer is turned off the clock keeps ticking.
There's no magic, just another machine someone glued on to your computer.

#Simple I/O

Computers need to be able to talk to other things to be useful, and IO
(input/output) is what we call the ways information travels in and out of a
computer.

Consider a Jacquard loom. A jacard loom is a textile machine that lets you make
complicated fabrics using punched cards. It does this by physically blocking an
armature, preventing a piece of colored thread from rising and appearing on the
"top" of a piece of fabric.

The input is a simple chain of punched cards, and the output is which arms are 
raised during that cycle.

Consider a player piano, which takes a scroll with holes in it, denoting when a
particular note should be played. As the scroll moves past a mechanism, hooks
catch on the punched holes, dragging the hammers upwards. Finally the hooks are
at an angle where they release, and the hammers fall, striking the piano string.

The input is a scoll representing what notes you want playes, and the output is
a falling hammer.

Computer IO follows similar patterns, except the actions that play out are more
complicated.
